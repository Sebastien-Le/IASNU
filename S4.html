Session 4: Comparing the scores of two habits using the t-test (2)

Objectives of this session

This session is crucial, as it introduces the concept of model. We try to make a transition from the comparison of two conditions to a model in which the conditions factor can explain the variability of the data.

Exercises

From the comparison of two means to the paired sample t-test and vice versa

In the previous session, we compared the scores of two habits by examining their means, without taking into account that the scores were collected from the same 167 assessors. To address this, the data needs to be paired, which necessitates modifying the structure of the dataset. In this new structure, the assessor becomes the statistical unit of interest. Let’s have a look at this new dataset. To do so, we apply the head() function to display the first rows of our dataset.

data_paired %>% head()
##   never_airplane_score unplug_score
## 1                    5            1
## 2                    3            2
## 3                    4            1
## 4                    4            0
## 5                    1            2
## 6                    5            1

Unlike the previous session, where the comparison involved one quantitative variable and two conditions, the current scenario entails comparing two quantitative variables measured on the same individuals. Hence, the syntax for the t.test() needs to incorporate the names of the two vectors of scores to be compared, along with specifying that the data is paired.

t.test(data_paired$never_airplane_score, data_paired$unplug_score, paired = TRUE)
## 
##  Paired t-test
## 
## data:  data_paired$never_airplane_score and data_paired$unplug_score
## t = 5.8044, df = 166, p-value = 3.199e-08
## alternative hypothesis: true mean difference is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  0.6796052 1.3802751
## sample estimates:
## mean difference 
##         1.02994

In the same way as before, it is easy to conclude about the significance of the difference between the two means.

To undestand how this paired sample t-test works, let’s calculate the vector of the differences between the variables data_paired$never_airplane_score and data_paired$unplug_score, and let’s run the t.test() function on this vector using the default options.

t.test(data_paired$never_airplane_score - data_paired$unplug_score)
## 
##  One Sample t-test
## 
## data:  data_paired$never_airplane_score - data_paired$unplug_score
## t = 5.8044, df = 166, p-value = 3.199e-08
## alternative hypothesis: true mean is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  0.6796052 1.3802751
## sample estimates:
## mean of x 
##   1.02994

As you can see, the two analyses provide the same results.

From the paired sample t-test to the notion of model

As we said, comparing the means associated with the two habits raises the question of whether the mean depends on the habits or not, which was expressed by the formula Score ~ Habit. Similarly, one could also ask whether the mean depends on the assessors or not. Ultimately, what we would like to test is the following hypothesis: Score ~ Habit + Judge. This can be done, as suggested by the syntax, using a linear model and the lm() function. Results of the lm() function are stored in an R object named res.lm. Then, the anova() function is applied to the object res.lm. The name of this function often leads to confusion or misunderstanding. Actually, this function computes analysis of variance (or deviance) tables for one or more fitted model objects. In our case, the fitted model object is the result of the lm() function.

res.lm <- lm(Score ~ Habit + Judge, data_plane_unplug)
anova(res.lm)
## Analysis of Variance Table
## 
## Response: Score
##            Df Sum Sq Mean Sq F value    Pr(>F)    
## Habit       1  88.57  88.575  33.691 3.199e-08 ***
## Judge     166 348.27   2.098   0.798    0.9265    
## Residuals 166 436.43   2.629                      
## ---
## Signif. codes:  0 '***' 0.001 '**' 0.01 '*' 0.05 '.' 0.1 ' ' 1

The results obtained from the anova() function are essential. Remember, in the previous session, when we compared two variances by calculating their ratio? It’s the same idea, here: we compare sum of squares divided by their degrees of freedom. The F value is the ratio of the sum of squares divided by their degrees of freedom. For the Habit variable, 33.691 = 88.575/2.629.

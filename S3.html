Session 3 : Comparing the scores of two habits using the t-test

Objectives of this session

Exercises

Remember that in the previous session we made our decision on the basis of a confidence interval, a distribution of averages, a number of steps relative to a reference situation: we can’t make a decision on the basis of a single value such as the average. Individual data are essential because we need to incorporate the notion of variability into our decision making.

In this session, for technical reasons that we will explain later, we will first look at how to compare two variances with each other, and then we will see how the results of this variance comparison can be used to compare two means with each other.

We will look at the habits of never flying on an airplane, and unplugging one’s electrical devices when you are not using it.

Comparing two variances

In order to filter out the data for scores for never flying an airplane, and unplugging one’s electrical devices, we will use the filter() function to filter out the habits that we are trying to compare and saving it to another data frame.

data_plane_unplug <- data_long_score %>% filter(Habit == "never_airplane_score"|
                                                Habit == "unplug_score")

Two values can be compared either by calculating their difference or by calculating their ratio. Comparing two variances is done by calculating the ratio of these two variances. Since the variance is a sum of squares, the ratio of two variances follows a certain distribution when the variances are equal.

For this comparison, we’ll use the same proof by contradiction that we used in the previous session. A proof of contradiction is a logical reasoning in which a proposition is proven true by assuming its opposite (contradiction) and then showing that this assumption leads to an absurd or contradictory result. This method helps establish the truth of the original proposition by showing that its negation cannot hold without leading to logical inconsistencies.

In our case, we’ll calculate the ratio of two variances and then check whether this ratio falls within the expected distribution when the variances are equal: that is the idea behind the very important F-test. Let’s compare our variances with the var.test() function.

Our dataset data_plane_unplug comprises three variables: one quantitative variable representing different scores, and two qualitative variables—one for identifying the habit and another for identifying the assessor. Comparing the variances associated with the two habits raises the question of whether the variance depends on the habits or not.

Thus, when using the var.test() function for comparing variances in R, you need to provide two arguments: the formula that expresses the relationship or dependency and the name of the dataset.

In the R programming language, the tilde character (~) is used to represent dependency or relationships between variables, especially in the context of modeling or formula specifications.

var.test(Score ~ Habit, data_plane_unplug)
## 
##  F test to compare two variances
## 
## data:  Score by Habit
## F = 1.2669, num df = 166, denom df = 166, p-value = 0.1285
## alternative hypothesis: true ratio of variances is not equal to 1
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  0.933576 1.719189
## sample estimates:
## ratio of variances 
##           1.266883

The output indicates that the default test compares the equality of variances to the scenario where variances are unequal, given that the ratio of variances differs from 1, our reference value. The confidence interval of the ratio of true variances contains our reference value 1. This suggests that, within the calculated confidence interval, there is no significant evidence to conclude that the variances are different from each other.

Using the proof of contradiction, the ratio we have observed based on our data falls within the expected distribution of the ratio of two variances when they are equal (p-value = 0.1285): this suggests that there is no substantial evidence to refute the assumption of equal variances based on the observed data.

Comparing two means

Similarly to the comparison of two variances, comparing the means associated with the two habits raises the question of whether the means depends on the habits or not. Therefore, we use the same syntax to compare two means as we do to compare two variances, but with the t.test() function, specifying that the comparison is made on the assumption that the variances are equal.

t.test(Score ~ Habit, var.equal = TRUE, data_plane_unplug)
## 
##  Two Sample t-test
## 
## data:  Score by Habit
## t = 6.1217, df = 332, p-value = 2.611e-09
## alternative hypothesis: true difference in means between group never_airplane_score and group unplug_score is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  0.6989826 1.3608976
## sample estimates:
## mean in group never_airplane_score         mean in group unplug_score 
##                           2.676647                           1.646707

As mentionned previously, two values can be compared either by calculating their difference or by calculating their ratio. Comparing two means is done by calculating their difference and comparing this difference to a reference value which is 0. This time, the confidence interval calculated is that around the difference between the true means. To obtain this interval, we need to estimate this difference and, as before, estimate the variance of the difference between the means. To estimate this variance, it is necessary to test for equality of variances, as was done previously.

Several elements of the output indicate that the difference between the true means is different from 0. The confidence interval around the true mean does not contain the reference value. The p-value is much smaller than 0.05, meaning that we can reject the null hypothesis, and accept the alternative hypothesis. All of this is, of course, consistent with the fact that what has been observed is far from the reference situation, the true means are equal, in terms of the number of standard deviations (t = 6.12).

Therefore, the perceptions of constraint of never taking an airplane, and unplugging one’s electric device are not equal. Looking at the mean values of each habit, we can assume that never riding an airplane is considered more constraining than unplugging your electrical devices.
